# Ogilvy Principles — Reference
Source: Ogilvy on Advertising (1983), Confessions of an Advertising Man (1963)

## The Non-Negotiables

### 1. The Big Idea
"Unless your advertising contains a Big Idea, it will pass like a ship in the night. I doubt if more than one campaign in a hundred contains a Big Idea."

A Big Idea is a concept so powerful it commands attention, differentiates the brand, and can sustain a campaign for years. Examples:
- Snickers: "You're not you when you're hungry"
- Rolls-Royce: "At 60mph, the loudest noise is the electric clock"
- Dove: "One-quarter moisturizing cream"
- VW: "Think small" (Bernbach, but Ogilvy-admired)

The Big Idea is NOT a tagline. It's a *perspective* — a reframe of how you see the product or the customer's problem.

### 2. Headline Primacy
"On average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. It follows that unless your headline sells your product, you have wasted 90% of your money."

Rules for headlines:
- Promise a benefit OR deliver news OR offer service OR tell a significant story OR recognize a problem
- Include the brand name when possible (80% of people who don't read body copy will never know the brand)
- Never use puns, double meanings, or obscurities — "this is a sin"
- Don't be clever at the expense of clarity
- Headlines below the illustration are read by 10% more people than headlines above it
- If you find a headline that works — keep using it

### 3. Benefit-Led Copy
"Advertising which promises no benefit to the consumer doesn't sell, yet the majority of campaigns contain no promise whatever."

The benefit extraction chain:
Feature → Benefit → Outcome → Emotion
Example: "256-bit encryption" → "your data stays private" → "no breach liability" → "sleep at night"

Ogilvy on P&G's formula (the gold standard):
- Always promise ONE important benefit
- First duty is to communicate effectively, not be original or entertaining
- Include a "moment of confirmation" (show the benefit being experienced)
- Talk directly to the consumer in their language

### 4. Specificity Kills Puffery
"Make your promise specific. Instead of generalities, use percentages, time elapsed, dollars saved."

The Rolls-Royce approach: 607 words of specific, factual copy. No adjectives. No "world-class." Just facts that let the reader conclude it's world-class themselves.

Test: replace every adjective with a number. If you can't, the claim is probably weak.

### 5. Research Before Writing
"The more you know about a product, the more likely you are to come up with a big idea for selling it."

Ogilvy's research process:
1. Study the product exhaustively (he spent 3 weeks reading about Rolls-Royce)
2. Research competitor advertising and its effectiveness
3. Research the consumer: how do they think? what language do they use? what would make them buy?

"Positioning is what the product does, and who it is for." — define this before writing a word.

### 6. One Objective, One Promise
"Most campaigns are too complicated. They reflect a long list of objectives and try to reconcile the divergent views of too many executives. By attempting to cover too many things, they achieve nothing."

The page/ad/campaign has ONE job. Everything serves that job. Cut everything that doesn't.

### 7. Long Copy for Complex Products
"Long copy sells more than short copy for products that require explanation."

This is explicitly relevant for B2B SaaS:
- Complex products need space to educate and overcome objections
- Readers who make it past the headline are interested — give them what they need
- The risk isn't writing too much — it's writing boringly
- "You can't bore people into buying"

### 8. Repeat the Benefit
"Opinion Research found that people who know a company well are 5× more likely to have a favorable opinion of it."

Repetition is not redundancy — the prospect is seeing your page for the first time. What you're tired of, they're just encountering. Restate the core benefit in the hero, in the proof section, and in the final CTA.

### 9. Respect the Reader's Intelligence
"The consumer isn't a moron; she is your wife."

Don't condescend. Don't oversimplify. Give facts, trust them to conclude. The Rolls-Royce ad worked because it respected the intelligence of its buyer.

### 10. Every Ad Is a Contribution to Brand
"Every advertisement should be thought of as a contribution to the complex symbol which is the brand image."

No page exists in isolation. Each touchpoint either reinforces or erodes the brand. Consistency over time is more powerful than any individual execution.

## Applied to Web/Landing Pages

| Ogilvy Era | Web Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Print headline | H1 / hero headline |
| Body copy | Section body text, feature descriptions |
| Illustration | Hero image/video, product screenshots |
| Caption | Image alt text, callout labels |
| Coupon / response mechanism | CTA button + form |
| Long copy | Long-form landing page, scrolling sections |
| Testimonial | Customer quote + logo bar |
| Factual claim | Metric callouts ("4× faster", "$2M saved") |
| Brand logo in headline | Product name in hero headline |

Key web adaptations:
- Scroll replaces the eye path of a print ad — structure the page like Ogilvy structured an ad
- The hero headline must work without the subhead (mobile, social previews)
- CTAs should be as direct as Ogilvy's "Mail this coupon today"
- Every section headline should be able to stand alone (scanners)
